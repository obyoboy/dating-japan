How to Spend New Year's Eve and New Year's Day with a Japanese Partner

New Year in Japan is deeply tied to family, tradition, and quiet reflection. Here's how to navigate oshogatsu with a Japanese partner and make it meaningful.

Last updated: 2026-04-10  |  6 min read

1. Why New Year Is a Big Deal in Japan

In Japan, New Year (お正月, oshogatsu) is the most important holiday of the year — bigger than Christmas. It is traditionally a time for family gatherings, reflection, and fresh starts. For a foreign partner, this can feel unfamiliar at first, but understanding what oshogatsu means culturally will help you participate with genuine respect.

Unlike Western New Year celebrations focused on parties and alcohol, Japanese New Year tends to be quieter, more spiritual, and centered on the home. Your Japanese partner may have long-standing family obligations during this period.

2. Countdown Night (大晦日 — Ōmisoka)

December 31 is called Ōmisoka. Many couples spend the evening together before or after family time. Popular options include watching the NHK Kōhaku Uta Gassen song contest on TV, visiting a countdown event in a city like Tokyo or Osaka, or simply sharing a warm meal at home.

Tip — If your partner watches Kōhaku with her family every year, offer to join them rather than pushing for a separate date. Joining family traditions early in a relationship is a meaningful gesture.

Temple bells (joya no kane) are rung 108 times at midnight at Buddhist temples across Japan. Attending together is a romantic and culturally rich experience — many temples allow visitors to ring the bell themselves if you line up early.

3. New Year's Day Traditions to Know

January 1 in Japan is calm and ceremonial. Shops and restaurants are often closed. Most families eat osechi ryōri — elaborately prepared traditional foods with symbolic meanings — and spend the day at home or at a local shrine.

4. Hatsumode — The First Shrine Visit

Visiting a shrine or temple for hatsumode is something most Japanese people do at least once during the first three days of January. This is an excellent date activity. Popular shrines such as Meiji Jingu in Tokyo or Fushimi Inari in Kyoto get extremely crowded — expect long lines if you go on January 1.

At hatsumode, couples typically draw omikuji (fortune slips) together, buy ema (wooden wishing plaques), and pray at the main hall. It is quiet, meaningful, and surprisingly romantic.

Tip — Ask your partner what shrine she usually goes to. Many people return to the same shrine every year out of habit or family tradition. Going to her shrine shows care and curiosity.

5. Navigating Family Time

The first few days of January are reserved for family in Japanese culture. If you are not yet close to her family, do not expect to spend January 1 together — especially in early stages of dating. She may visit her parents or grandparents, and that takes priority.

Warning — Do not pressure your Japanese partner to skip family New Year obligations to spend time with you. This can create real conflict and signal that you do not respect her cultural duties. Plan your couple time around her schedule, not against it.

If you are in a serious relationship, New Year may be the time you are introduced to her family. Take this seriously — dress neatly, bring a small gift (omiyage), and learn basic New Year greetings such as Akemashite omedetō gozaimasu (Happy New Year).

6. Couple Date Ideas Around New Year

There are plenty of ways to celebrate as a couple even within the oshogatsu framework. Here are some ideas that work well with Japanese New Year culture:

Evening of December 31: Visit a countdown event at a temple or public square, watch Kōhaku together, or cook a simple meal at home with Japanese New Year foods like toshikoshi soba (year-crossing noodles).

January 2 or 3: These days are often more flexible than January 1. Go for hatsumode together, try fukubukuro shopping, or take a short day trip to a scenic winter location.

January 2 evening: Many couples go to the movies — hatsuyume (first dream of the year) and New Year films are a cultural touchstone. City shopping areas also reopen around this time.

7. Simple Language to Know

Even a few Japanese New Year phrases will be warmly received by your partner and her family. Showing effort with language during important holidays carries real weight in Japanese relationships.

Tip — Say Akemashite omedetō gozaimasu on January 1 itself, and Yoi otoshi o before midnight on December 31. Using them correctly will impress even native speakers.

8. Making It Meaningful for Both of You

New Year in Japan is a time of sincerity. It is not about grand gestures — it is about presence, care, and shared quiet moments. If you approach oshogatsu with genuine curiosity and respect for its traditions, your Japanese partner will feel that.

Ask questions, participate in what she enjoys, and do not try to impose Western party culture onto a holiday that means something different here. The couples who handle New Year well in Japan are usually the ones who listen first and plan second.
