Dating App Scams in Japan: What Foreign Men Need to Know

Dating apps in Japan can be great for meeting people — but scammers know foreigners are a prime target. Here's how to protect yourself.

Last updated: 2026-04-10  |  7 min read

1. Why Foreigners Are Targeted on Japanese Dating Apps

Foreigners using dating apps in Japan are frequently targeted by scammers. The reasons are straightforward: you may be less familiar with local norms, less likely to recognize suspicious patterns in Japanese, and sometimes more emotionally vulnerable — especially if you've recently arrived and are hoping to make meaningful connections.

Scammers know this. They craft profiles specifically designed to appeal to foreign men: attractive photos, fluent or near-fluent English, and a story that fits what many foreigners hope to find. Understanding this targeting is the first step in defending yourself.

3. Red Flags on Profiles and Early Conversations

Most scammers reveal themselves through patterns that feel off once you know what to look for. Pay attention to these warning signs in profiles and early messages.

  • Too-perfect photos: Professional-grade images, no casual or candid shots, or photos that reverse-image search to stock photo sites or foreign social media accounts.
  • Vague or inconsistent backstory: They claim to be half-Japanese, work overseas, or have an unusual living situation that explains why they can't meet easily.
  • Immediate English fluency: A person living in Japan whose first language is Japanese but writes flawless, colloquial English without any awkwardness.
  • Pushes to move off-platform quickly: Within the first few messages, they want your LINE ID, WhatsApp, or email.
  • Avoids video calls: Consistently has a reason not to video chat — bad connection, camera broken, shy — despite chatting daily.
Tip Reverse image search any profile photo using Google Images or TinEye before investing time in a conversation. Right-click the image or save it and upload it. A scammer's photo will often appear on other sites under a different name.

4. The Long-Game Romance Scam

The most emotionally damaging scam is the long-game romance fraud, sometimes called "pig butchering" (sha zhu pan). The scammer invests weeks or even months building a genuine-feeling relationship. They're attentive, sweet, and consistent. Then, once trust is established, they introduce a cryptocurrency platform or investment opportunity where you "can't lose."

Initial small "investments" appear to pay off. You're shown convincing dashboards. When you try to withdraw, fees or taxes are invented. Victims have lost tens of thousands of dollars this way. The emotional betrayal is severe because the relationship itself felt real.

Warning If someone you met on a dating app mentions cryptocurrency, forex trading, or any investment platform — especially one you've never heard of — treat it as a scam immediately. No legitimate romantic interest needs to discuss investment opportunities in early conversations. Block and report.

5. Which Apps See the Most Scam Activity

Scammers exist on all platforms, but certain app structures attract more of them. Free apps with low verification barriers — including some international apps used in Japan — have higher scam rates. Apps that require payment or stricter identity verification tend to have fewer fake profiles.

Japanese-domestic apps like Pairs and Omiai require real name registration and age verification via official ID, which significantly reduces fake accounts. However, no app is completely scam-free, and scammers do create accounts on paid apps as well. Stay vigilant regardless of platform.

6. How to Protect Yourself

Practical steps you can take to reduce your risk significantly:

  • Use apps with identity verification — Pairs, Omiai, and Tapple require it.
  • Never send money or gift cards to someone you haven't met in person multiple times.
  • Keep early conversations on the app itself — don't rush to LINE or WhatsApp.
  • Do a video call before agreeing to meet — use it to confirm the person matches their photos.
  • Tell a friend you trust about the person you're talking to — outside perspective helps you see red flags you might be rationalizing.
  • If an investment topic comes up, end the conversation.
Tip Japan's National Police Agency (NPA) and the Consumer Affairs Agency both accept reports of online fraud. If you've been scammed, report it — it helps others and can sometimes assist in recovery. You can also contact your embassy if significant money was lost.

7. What to Do If You've Been Scammed

If you realize you've been targeted, don't be embarrassed — these operations are run by organized criminal groups with professional scripts and trained operators. Being deceived does not reflect badly on your intelligence.

Stop all contact immediately. Do not send more money in hopes of recovering what you've lost — this is a further manipulation tactic called "recovery scamming." Document all conversations, screenshots, and any transaction records. Report to the dating app, your bank if money was transferred, and local police. If you're in Japan, you can report to the nearest police station or via the NPA's online fraud reporting portal.
