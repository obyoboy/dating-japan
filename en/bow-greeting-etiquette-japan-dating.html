Bowing and Greeting Etiquette for a First Date in Japan

A respectful first greeting can set the right tone on a date in Japan. Learn how to bow naturally, what to say, and how to avoid awkward mistakes.

Last updated: 2026-04-24  |  6 min read

1. Start with a simple, respectful greeting

When you meet a Japanese date for the first time, a calm greeting matters more than being overly impressive. A polite smile, light eye contact, and a short greeting such as “Hajimemashite” if it is your first meeting, or “Konnichiwa” if you have already exchanged messages, is usually enough. Speak clearly and at a normal volume.

You do not need to perform a dramatic bow. In dating settings, a natural, moderate bow often feels more comfortable than something exaggerated. The goal is to show sincerity, not to look formal in a stiff way.

Tip If you are nervous, keep your greeting short and steady. A simple “Nice to meet you” in English, paired with a polite bow, is often better than forcing unfamiliar Japanese phrases.

2. How to bow naturally

A basic bow is simple: stand straight, keep your hands at your sides or neatly in front of you, and bend from the hips with your back straight. For a casual first date, a small bow of about 15 degrees is usually appropriate. Hold it briefly, then rise smoothly.

If you are meeting in a more formal setting, such as a nice restaurant or a family introduction, the bow may be slightly deeper. For a casual date at a café or station, keep it light. The key is consistency and calmness, not perfection.

3. Common greeting phrases and what they mean

There is no need to memorize many lines. A few basic phrases are enough for a respectful first impression. “Hajimemashite” means “Nice to meet you,” and “Yoroshiku onegaishimasu” is a polite phrase that can be used when greeting someone for the first time. If you are leaving, “Arigatou gozaimashita” is a polite thank you.

It is fine to use English if your Japanese is limited, especially if your date also prefers English. What matters is that you speak gently and show appreciation for the meeting. A warm tone is often more important than perfect grammar.

Warning Avoid joking about bowing, copying anime-style gestures, or making the greeting overly theatrical. That can feel disrespectful or make the moment awkward.

4. Body language that supports a good first impression

Your posture, facial expression, and timing all affect how your greeting feels. Stand with relaxed shoulders, avoid crossing your arms, and do not crowd the other person. Give a little space when you say hello and bow. If you are carrying a bag, keep it out of the way before you greet her.

Also pay attention to timing. If she bows first, return the bow naturally. If she offers a handshake instead, follow her lead. Many Japanese women will appreciate a foreign date who adapts calmly instead of insisting on one style.

Eye contact is good, but do not stare. A brief look, a smile, and then a polite greeting is usually the most comfortable approach.

5. What to avoid on a first meeting

Some actions can create a bad first impression even if you mean well. A very deep bow can feel excessive in a casual dating setting. A casual wave without any greeting can feel too relaxed if you are meeting for the first time. Speaking too loudly, leaning in too close, or trying to show off your Japanese can also create pressure.

It is also wise to avoid assuming every Japanese woman expects the same etiquette. Preferences differ by age, personality, and context. When in doubt, stay polite and moderate. That approach works in most situations.

Tip If you are unsure, mirror her level of formality. A gentle bow, a warm smile, and a respectful tone are usually safe and appreciated.

6. Ending the greeting smoothly

After the first greeting, move into the date naturally. You can say something like, “I’m happy to meet you,” then suggest walking to the café, restaurant, or meeting spot. The transition should feel relaxed, not like a ceremony.

If the date goes well, a polite goodbye matters too. A small bow and a sincere “Thank you for today” can leave a strong positive impression. In Japanese dating, thoughtful manners often speak louder than big gestures.

When you show respect without overdoing it, your greeting becomes a sign of confidence rather than anxiety. That is usually the best start to a first date in Japan.
