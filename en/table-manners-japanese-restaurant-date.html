Table Manners You Must Know for a Japanese Restaurant Date

Dining out in Japan is more than just eating — it's a ritual. Knowing the unwritten rules before your date can mean the difference between a great impression and an awkward evening.

Last updated: 2026-04-10  |  6 min read

1. Why Table Manners Matter More in Japan

Japanese dining culture places a strong emphasis on consideration for others and respect for food. On a date, your table behavior signals not just upbringing, but how you treat people around you. A woman who grew up in Japan has absorbed these norms since childhood — and she will notice when they are broken, even if she says nothing.

This does not mean you must be perfect. Most Japanese women dating foreign men extend grace for cultural differences. But showing that you have made an effort goes a long way.

2. The Right Way to Use Chopsticks

Chopsticks are central to Japanese dining, and there are specific things you should never do with them.

ActionWhy It Matters
Sticking chopsticks upright in riceResembles a funeral offering — very bad omen
Passing food chopstick-to-chopstickMimics a bone-passing ceremony at cremations
Pointing with chopsticksConsidered rude and childish
Spearing food with chopsticksShows lack of basic table skills
Waving chopsticks while thinkingSeen as distracting and impolite

If you are still learning to use chopsticks, it is perfectly fine to ask for a fork — but make a small effort with chopsticks first. It shows willingness, which she will appreciate.

Tip Practice handling chopsticks before the date. Even basic proficiency will earn you genuine points.

3. Saying It Right: Itadakimasu and Gochisousama

Two phrases bookend every meal in Japan. Itadakimasu (いただきます) is said before eating — it expresses gratitude for the food and to everyone who prepared it. Gochisousama deshita (ごちそうさまでした) is said after finishing, thanking the host or restaurant.

Using these phrases at the right moments will immediately show cultural awareness. You do not need to say them loudly — a quiet, sincere delivery is more appropriate than a performative one.

4. Pouring Drinks: Her Glass First, Never Yours

In Japanese dining etiquette, you pour for others — not for yourself. If you are sharing a bottle of beer or sake, pour her glass first, then let her pour yours. Pouring your own drink before hers reads as self-centered.

Keep an eye on her glass throughout the meal. Topping it up before it is completely empty is a small gesture that communicates attentiveness.

Tip When she pours for you, hold your glass slightly elevated with both hands as a sign of respect. This small gesture often surprises and delights Japanese women who are not expecting it from a foreigner.

5. Noise, Slurping, and What Is Actually Acceptable

You may have heard that slurping noodles is acceptable in Japan — and it is, particularly with ramen or soba. It signals enjoyment. However, this applies specifically to noodles, not to soups or other dishes.

Blowing your nose at the table is considered far more offensive than slurping noodles. If you need to, excuse yourself and go to the restroom. Similarly, talking with your mouth full, reaching across someone's plate, or making loud eating sounds beyond noodle slurping are all things to avoid.

Warning Do not blow your nose at the table under any circumstances — this is considered highly impolite in Japanese dining culture and will likely make her uncomfortable.

6. Paying the Bill: What She Expects

There is no single universal rule here, but on a first date in Japan, many women still expect the man to offer to pay. This is especially true in more traditional restaurant settings. That said, a growing number of younger Japanese women prefer to split or pay their share — known as warikan (割り勘).

A good approach: offer to pay, and read her reaction. If she insists on splitting, accept graciously. Never make a scene over the bill or draw attention to it. Handle payment quietly and efficiently.

SituationRecommended Approach
First date, traditional restaurantOffer to pay; let her respond naturally
Casual lunch or coffeeSplitting is more commonly expected
She insists on paying her shareAccept without protest — she means it
She seems to expect you to payPay without hesitation or commentary

7. Handling the Oshibori and Other Small Rituals

Many Japanese restaurants provide an oshibori — a small damp towel for wiping your hands before eating. Use it only on your hands, not your face. After use, fold it neatly and set it aside rather than leaving it crumpled.

Other small things to keep in mind: do not stack your dishes when finished (staff prefer to clear them properly), keep your phone face-down or put away during the meal, and do not tip — tipping is not customary in Japan and can sometimes cause confusion or embarrassment.

Tip Putting your phone away during the entire meal sends a strong message that she has your full attention. This simple act is one of the most effective things you can do on a Japanese date.
