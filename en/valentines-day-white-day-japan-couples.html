Valentine's Day and White Day in Japan: What Couples Actually Do

Japan's take on Valentine's Day flips the script — and White Day one month later completes the cycle. Here's what couples actually do and what's expected of you.

Last updated: 2026-04-10  |  6 min read

1. How Valentine's Day Works in Japan

In Japan, Valentine's Day on February 14 is celebrated quite differently from Western countries. Traditionally, it is the women who give chocolate to men — not the other way around. This custom developed in the 1950s and has remained a defining feature of the holiday ever since.

There are two main types of chocolate given: honmei-choco (本命チョコ), meaning "true feeling" chocolate given to a romantic partner or crush, and giri-choco (義理チョコ), or "obligation" chocolate given to coworkers, friends, or bosses out of social courtesy. As a foreigner in a relationship with a Japanese woman, you will almost certainly receive honmei-choco — a sincere and meaningful gesture.

2. What She Expects From You on Valentine's Day

Many Japanese women are aware that in Western culture, Valentine's Day involves men giving gifts too. If your girlfriend has international experience or is familiar with global customs, she may expect a card, flowers, or a small gift from you on February 14. Even without that expectation, making a gesture on Valentine's Day shows thoughtfulness and cultural awareness.

Tip If you are unsure what she expects, a heartfelt card or a small bouquet goes a long way. Ask her casually beforehand — Japanese women often appreciate that you care enough to ask rather than guess.

3. White Day: The Return Gift on March 14

Exactly one month after Valentine's Day, on March 14, Japan celebrates White Day. This is the day when men who received chocolate on Valentine's Day are expected to give a return gift. White Day was created by confectionery companies in the late 1970s and has become a well-established tradition.

The expectation is that your return gift should be worth roughly two to three times the value of what you received — a concept sometimes called sanbai gaeshi (三倍返し). Common White Day gifts include white chocolate, marshmallows, cookies, jewelry, accessories, or other sweets.

4. Handmade vs. Store-Bought Chocolate

A common tradition among Japanese women is making homemade chocolate for their significant other on Valentine's Day. If your girlfriend gives you handmade chocolate, treat it as a significant act of affection — it takes effort and signals genuine feeling. Reacting with genuine appreciation matters more than any gift you could buy.

Store-bought chocolate from premium brands like Godiva, Royce, or local patisseries is also very popular and perfectly respectable. Many department stores set up special Valentine's Day sections weeks in advance, and shopping for honmei-choco has become a social ritual in itself.

5. Planning a Date Around These Holidays

Both Valentine's Day and White Day are popular occasions for couples to go on dates. Restaurants, cafes, and hotels often offer special menus or packages. Reservations fill up quickly — especially in Tokyo and Osaka — so booking ahead by at least two weeks is strongly advised.

Warning Do not assume she wants an elaborate dinner if your relationship is still new. Some Japanese women prefer a quieter, more private celebration. Read the relationship stage and plan accordingly — over-the-top gestures can feel overwhelming early on.

6. Regional and Generational Differences

Attitudes toward Valentine's Day and White Day vary by generation and region. Younger women in urban areas often embrace both days enthusiastically, while women from more rural or traditional backgrounds may observe the holidays more quietly. The giri-choco tradition is also declining among younger generations, with many women opting out of workplace obligation chocolate altogether.

As a foreigner, you are not expected to know every nuance — but showing that you made an effort to understand and participate in these traditions signals respect for her culture and matters to most Japanese women.

7. Making These Holidays Meaningful

The most important thing is not the price of the gift but the thought behind it. A carefully chosen White Day gift that reflects something she mentioned wanting, a favorite flavor of sweets, or a small piece of jewelry tied to a shared memory will be remembered far longer than an expensive but impersonal purchase.

Use these holidays as opportunities to strengthen your connection — write a note in your message, reference an inside joke, or pair the gift with a plan for something you will do together. That combination of effort and personalization is what makes these Japanese holiday traditions genuinely special for couples.
