What Is a Gokon? How Foreigners Can Join a Group Blind Date in Japan

A gokon is Japan's popular group blind date format — and it's one of the best ways for foreigners to meet Japanese women in a relaxed social setting. Here's everything you need to know.

Last updated: 2026-04-10  |  6 min read

1. What Is a Gokon?

A gokon (合コン) is a structured group blind date that typically brings together an equal number of men and women — usually three to five people on each side — at a restaurant, izakaya, or bar. The word combines gō (合, mixed) and kon (コン, short for konpa, a social party). It is one of the most common ways young Japanese adults meet potential romantic partners outside of work or school.

Unlike a Western singles mixer, a gokon is almost always organized through mutual friends. One person from each side agrees to gather their friends, and both groups meet for the first time at the venue. The atmosphere is casual and the goal is simply to enjoy conversation — though numbers are often exchanged afterward if there is a mutual interest.

2. How a Gokon Typically Works

Understanding the format helps you behave naturally and make a good impression. Here is what usually happens from start to finish.

PhaseWhat Happens
ArrivalBoth groups arrive around the same time. Everyone introduces themselves by first name.
SeatingMen and women alternate seats. Organizers sometimes shuffle halfway through.
OrderingAll-you-can-drink (nomihōdai) courses are common. Orders are placed together.
ConversationLight topics — hobbies, travel, food. Avoid heavy or controversial subjects early on.
Bill SplitCosts are usually split evenly by gender group, sometimes with men paying slightly more.
After-partySmaller groups may go to a second venue (nijikai). This is where connections deepen.

3. Can Foreigners Join a Gokon?

Yes — and foreigners are sometimes specifically invited because some participants find it exciting to meet someone from abroad. That said, gokon are social events built on trust and personal networks, so the most reliable path is through a Japanese friend who can vouch for you.

If you have Japanese coworkers, university classmates, or friends from a language exchange, let them know you are interested in social gatherings. Many Japanese people who regularly attend gokon are happy to include a foreign friend once they trust you and know your personality.

Tip Language exchange meetups (言語交換) and international friendship circles are excellent places to build the Japanese friendships that lead to gokon invitations. Apps like Meetup, Tandem, or HelloTalk can help you start those connections.

4. What to Expect as a Foreigner

Your foreign background will come up — that is natural. Be ready to answer questions about your home country, why you came to Japan, how long you plan to stay, and whether you speak Japanese. Answers that show genuine love for Japan and long-term commitment tend to land well.

Japanese women at a gokon are generally polite and will engage even if they are shy. Do not mistake politeness for romantic interest, and do not push the conversation in an overly personal direction early on. Building warmth and humor over the evening is the goal.

Warning Showing off, dominating conversation, or pressuring someone to drink more than they want are serious social missteps. Gokon culture rewards attentiveness and lighthearted charm, not loud bravado.

5. How to Behave at a Gokon

A few practical rules will help you blend in and make a positive impression:

Pour drinks for others before yourself. In Japanese drinking culture, you fill your neighbor's glass before your own. Do this naturally and people will notice.

Learn a few key phrases. Even basic Japanese — kanpai (cheers), oishii (delicious), tanoshii (fun) — shows respect and gets a warm reaction.

Dress neatly. Gokon participants generally dress smart-casual. Avoid anything too casual like athletic wear, but you do not need to be formal either.

Be attentive to the whole group. Talk to everyone, not only the person you are most attracted to. Monopolizing one person early in the evening can feel awkward for everyone.

6. Exchanging Contacts After a Gokon

If you are interested in someone, the most natural time to exchange LINE IDs (Japan's dominant messaging app) is near the end of the evening or during the nijikai. A simple "Let's keep in touch — can I get your LINE?" works. You can say it in English if your Japanese is limited; most participants will understand.

Do not expect an immediate answer on whether there is mutual interest. Japanese women often prefer to reflect and communicate by message rather than give a direct answer in person. A friendly follow-up message the next day — thanking her for the evening and referencing something specific you talked about — is a good way to continue the connection.

7. Alternatives If You Cannot Join a Gokon

If you do not yet have the social network to get invited to a gokon, there are related formats worth exploring. Machicon (街コン) are large-scale town-based mixer events open to the public — no invitation needed, and foreigners are welcome. International parties hosted at bars in Tokyo, Osaka, and other cities serve a similar purpose in a more open environment. Over time, attending these events builds the Japanese friendships that can eventually lead to a gokon invitation.
