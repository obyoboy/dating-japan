Drinking Culture and Alcohol Etiquette on Dates in Japan

Alcohol plays a unique social role in Japan. Whether you're heading to an izakaya or a cocktail bar, understanding Japanese drinking etiquette can make or break your first date.

Last updated: 2026-04-10  |  6 min read

1. Why Drinking Matters in Japanese Dating Culture

In Japan, sharing a drink is often less about the alcohol itself and more about the social ritual it represents. Going for a drink — nomi ni ikou (飲みに行こう) — is one of the most common ways Japanese people suggest a casual date or outing without the pressure of calling it a formal date.

This low-stakes framing makes izakayas (Japanese gastropubs) a popular first-date venue. The relaxed atmosphere, shared small plates, and steady stream of drinks create natural conversation flow and reduce awkwardness.

2. The Izakaya: Japan's Ideal First Date Venue

If your date suggests an izakaya, that's a good sign — it means she's comfortable and wants to spend time with you in a relaxed setting. Izakayas are affordable, casual, and designed for groups or couples to linger over food and drinks.

Tip When entering an izakaya, wait to be seated rather than choosing your own table. Most have private booth areas (半個室) that offer more privacy — great for a first date conversation.

Expect to order a round of drinks shortly after sitting down. The default opening drink is often beer (nama biru), but non-alcoholic options like oolong tea or soft drinks are always available and completely normal to order.

3. The Etiquette of the First Pour

One of the most visible drinking customs in Japan is not pouring your own drink. You pour for your companion, and they pour for you. On a first date, this small act signals attentiveness and care.

Making a toastSay kanpai (乾杯) and clink glasses at about chest height

Pouring for yourself repeatedly without offering to pour for your date reads as inconsiderate. Even if you're nervous, make a habit of checking her glass first.

4. Pacing Yourself: Drinking Too Much Too Fast

Japanese drinking culture generally values a steady, social pace rather than drinking quickly to get drunk. On a first date especially, getting visibly drunk reflects poorly and can make your date uncomfortable.

Warning Drinking heavily on a first date is a major red flag in Japanese dating culture. It signals poor self-control and can make your date feel unsafe or embarrassed. Pace yourself — the goal is connection, not intoxication.

If you find yourself drinking faster than your date, slow down naturally by focusing on the food or conversation. There is no pressure to match drink-for-drink, and most Japanese women will not expect or want that.

5. If You Don't Drink Alcohol

Not drinking alcohol is completely respected in Japan. You will never be pressured to drink, and non-alcoholic options are always available. Simply say watashi wa osake ga nomenai no de, sofuto dorinku de ii desu ka? (I can't drink alcohol — is a soft drink okay?) or just order a juice or tea without explanation.

What matters is that you still participate in the kanpai ritual. Pick up whatever drink is in front of you — even water — and join the toast. Sitting with empty hands during a kanpai feels socially awkward in Japan.

Tip Mentioning early in the date that you don't drink, and doing so cheerfully and without over-explaining, actually signals maturity and self-awareness. Most Japanese women will respect this without any issue.

6. Paying the Bill at a Drinking Date

At an izakaya, drinks and food are ordered throughout the meal, so the bill accumulates over time. It is common for the man to offer to pay on a first date, though many younger Japanese women today will offer to split or cover their share. Read the situation — if she insists, let her contribute gracefully rather than making a scene about it.

A practical approach: offer to pay, and if she resists, suggest splitting by saying something like jaa, warikan ni shimashou (let's split it then). This avoids either party feeling uncomfortable.

7. Reading the Atmosphere at the End of the Night

Izakaya dates can run long if the conversation is good. There is a concept in Japan called niji-kai (二次会) — the after-party, or moving to a second venue after the first. If your date suggests niji-kai, it is generally a strong sign the date went well.

When leaving, thank the staff together and walk her to the train station. Offering to see her home or to the station is a thoughtful gesture that will be noticed and appreciated.
